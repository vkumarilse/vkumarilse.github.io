Aims

This project examines Financial Inclusion in India, through Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana (PMJDY), the world's largest financial inclusion programme, through interactive data visualisation.

Three research questions guide the analysis:

  1. Geographic variation: How does PMJDY coverage vary across states, and what explains regional patterns?
  2. Access versus engagement: Does account ownership translate into active financial participation, measured through deposits and UPI adoption?
  3. Gender equity: How have gender gaps evolved, and where do disparities persist?

The analysis integrates state-wise PMJDY data (2015–2025), gender-disaggregated account statistics (2019–2024), and UPI transaction volumes to assess whether India has achieved substantive inclusion, where citizens actively use accounts, rather than merely formal access. India's account ownership rose from 35% (2011) to 89% (2024) according to World Bank Findex data, among the fastest expansions globally. This project tries to tease out whether such headline figures translate into meaningful financial inclusion at the state level.

Geographic Coverage of Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana (PMJDY)

The Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana (PMJDY), launched in 2014, is the world's largest financial inclusion programme, having opened over 500 million bank accounts for previously unbanked citizens.

Key Finding
Coverage ranges from 6.8 in Ladakh to 69.8 in Assam : a 10× difference. Eastern/central states lead penetration, while southern states with higher pre-existing banking access show lower PMJDY uptake.

Technical Note

Implementation: Direct Vega-Lite specification with embedded GeoJSON and PMJDY data.

  • Boundary challenges: J&K bifurcation (2019) created Ladakh UT; standardized GeoJSON definitions were inconsistent across sources
  • State name variations: Odisha/Orissa, Uttarakhand/Uttaranchal required manual reconciliation between RBI data and geographic boundaries
  • Coordinate systems: CRS mismatches between Web Mercator (EPSG:3857) and WGS84 (EPSG:4326) required precise projection calibration
  • Population projections: 2011 Census data projected to 2024 using state-specific growth rates; banking data from RBI PMJDY Progress Reports (Dec 2024)
  • Data embedding: All geographic and statistical data embedded in specification for reproducibility and offline rendering

Gender-Wise Account Distribution of PMJDY Accounts (2019-24)

Overview

Female participation ranges from 46.4% to 58.5% across states: a 12.1 percentage point gap. Tamil Nadu and Meghalaya lead (58.5%) driven by extensive microfinance and SHG networks and state-level incentive schemes. Urban centers (Delhi 48.1%, Chandigarh 46.4%) surprisingly lag behind, suggesting pre-existing banking was male-skewed.

Key Insights

  • Culture > Development: Kerala and Meghalaya both 58.5% despite different HDI
  • SHG correlation: States with good Self-Help Group networks rank highest
  • Urban paradox: Metropolitan areas show lower female shares

Data Limitations

Critical gap: No public data on transactions, balances, or credit access by gender limits assessment of actual financial inclusion vs account opening.

  • No transaction data (active vs dormant accounts unknown)
  • No gender-disaggregated balances (savings behavior unknown)
  • No credit uptake data (Overdraft Facility usage unknown)
  • Gender distribution data is not available for all years, the above data has been extracted from a question answered in House of Parliament in India.

PMJDY Coverage vs UPI Adoption

A State-Level Analysis of PMJDY and UPI Correlation

Overview: From Bank Accounts to Digital Transactions

The Unified Payments Interface (UPI) is India's real-time mobile payment system developed by the National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI). Launched in 2016, UPI enables instant fund transfers between bank accounts using a smartphone, and has since become the backbone of India's digital payments ecosystem, processing over 20 billion transactions monthly by late 2025.

This analysis examines the relationship between financial inclusion (PMJDY) and digital payment adoption (UPI) across 35 Indian states and union territories. Using state-wise data from official government sources, we employ correlation analysis and regression modelling to quantify this relationship. Our findings reveal a strong positive association: states with higher PMJDY penetration tend to exhibit greater UPI transaction volumes, though the relationship is mediated significantly by urbanisation. The log-log regression model achieves an R² of 0.90, indicating that a 1% increase in PMJDY account balances is associated with a 1.15% increase in UPI transaction volume, suggesting that active financial engagement, rather than mere account opening, drives digital payment adoption.

Correlation Matrix

Findings

The Pearson correlation matrix reveals several important patterns. UPI Volume and UPI Value are nearly perfectly correlated (r = 0.99), as expected. PMJDY Balance shows stronger correlation with UPI metrics (r = 0.63–0.66) than raw account counts (r = 0.56–0.58), suggesting that active account usage matters more than account opening alone. Most notably, Urban Accounts exhibit the strongest relationship with UPI adoption (r = 0.82–0.84), while Rural Accounts show substantially weaker correlations (r = 0.46–0.48). This urban-rural disparity highlights a persistent digital divide: financial inclusion has succeeded in rural penetration, but digital payment adoption remains concentrated in urban areas.

Urbanisation Effect and State Clusters

Urbanisation emerges as a critical mediator of the PMJDY-UPI relationship. States with higher urban PMJDY account ratios demonstrate significantly greater UPI adoption, independent of total account numbers. K-means clustering identifies four distinct state groups: "High Digital Leaders" (Maharashtra, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu), "Urban-Centric" states (Delhi, Chandigarh), "Large Emerging States" with high PMJDY but moderate UPI (Bihar, Uttar Pradesh), and "Early Stage" smaller states. This classification suggests differentiated policy approaches may be needed to accelerate digital payment adoption across diverse state contexts.

Data Sources & Methodology

PMJDY data sourced from pmjdy.gov.in (December 2025); UPI state-wise statistics from NPCI Ecosystem Statistics (November 2025). Analysis performed using Python (pandas, scipy, statsmodels, scikit-learn). Visualisations rendered with Vega-Lite v5.

Limitations: Approximately 42% of UPI transactions are classified as "unclassified" in NPCI data due to missing location information, which may affect state-level estimates. Cross-sectional analysis cannot establish causality; the relationship between PMJDY and UPI adoption may be confounded by unobserved state-level characteristics such as digital infrastructure quality or smartphone penetration rates.

Conclusions

The analysis reveals a nuanced picture of India's financial inclusion. PMJDY has achieved remarkable scale:572 million accounts, ₹2.8 lakh crore in deposits, but coverage patterns expose structural variations.

Regional Divergence

Eastern states (Assam: 69.8%, Bihar: 51.2%) show highest penetration, while southern states with pre-existing banking infrastructure show lower PMJDY uptake. This counterintuitive pattern reflects targeting of previously unbanked populations, not programme weakness.

Access-Engagement Gap

Our regression analysis (R²=0.90) demonstrates that PMJDY balances, not raw account counts, predict UPI adoption. A 1% increase in account balances associates with 1.15% higher UPI volume. Urbanisation mediates this relationship: urban accounts correlate with UPI at r=0.84 versus r=0.47 for rural accounts.

Gender Progress

Women hold 56% of PMJDY accounts : a significant achievement. However, female participation ranges from 46% (Chandigarh) to 58.5% (Tamil Nadu), with urban centres paradoxically lagging. Data limitations preclude assessing whether women use accounts actively.

Policy implication: Converting dormant accounts into active financial participation—particularly bridging the urban-rural digital divide, represents the critical "last mile" challenge for India's inclusion agenda.
